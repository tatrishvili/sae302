Social Media and Adolescent Mental Health
Dr. Edmond Manou√©lian, Psychiatrist at Camille Miret Institute
In this article, Dr. Edmond Manou√©lian, a psychiatrist, explains that social media has profoundly transformed the way adolescents experience their social relationships, particularly when confronted with harassment and its psychological consequences.
Transformation of Social Relationships
Social media has profoundly changed the way adolescents experience their relationships, amplifying social tensions and situations of harassment that were previously limited to school environments.
The Continuity of Cyberbullying
Unlike traditional school bullying, which could end once the student returned home, cyberbullying never stops. Messages, comments, photos, and humiliating videos can be shared at any time, following the victim everywhere. As a result, adolescents no longer have a safe space, which intensifies stress, fear, and feelings of helplessness.
Social Exclusion and Invisibility
Today, not owning a phone or not being present on social media can lead to marginalization. Adolescents who do not participate in online exchanges may be left out of discussions, groups, and events, reinforcing feelings of isolation and social invisibility.
Psychological Consequences
Constant exposure to the judgment of others can cause chronic anxiety, sleep disorders, decreased self-esteem, and in the most severe cases, depression. When misused or poorly supervised, social media becomes an aggravating factor in adolescent distress.
An Hyper-Awareness of the Self That Amplifies Young People's Distress
University of Rouen Normandy
This article adopts a sociological and psychological approach, explaining that social media is not merely a communication tool but acts as a "distorting mirror" of reality that influences how young people perceive themselves.
Social Media as Distorting Mirrors
Social media does not reflect reality but presents an idealized version of life. Young people are constantly exposed to perfect bodies, happy lives, and staged success. This constant comparison creates a hyper-awareness of the self, where adolescents observe and judge their own lives through the lens of social media.
Comparison and Feelings of Inadequacy
The constant comparison with staged lives creates feelings of inadequacy and continuous pressure to appear happy and perfect. This can lead to the impression of never being good enough or happy enough, generating deep psychological distress.
Emotional Desensitization
Repeated exposure to violent or intimate content trivializes emotions and blurs the boundary between private life and public space. Taboos disappear, and the sense of what is appropriate becomes distorted.
Accepting Distress as Normal
Researchers emphasize that distress is part of life. True mental health does not mean feeling good all the time. The danger of social media lies in the desire to measure, compare, and quantify everything, which prevents acceptance of moments of vulnerability. We must "depathologize" everyday life, accepting that occasional distress is normal and healthy.
The Impact of Social Media on Self-Esteem and Identity
Prof. R.J. Starr, The Psychology of Us
This article focuses on the psychological mechanisms at work in social media and their influence on identity construction, particularly among adolescents and young adults seeking constant validation online.
Emotional Validation and Dopamine Rewards
Social media functions as a system of emotional validation. Each like, comment, or reaction acts as a psychological reward. These interactions activate the brain's pleasure circuits, notably through the release of dopamine, making users dependent on others' approval.
The Fragility of Self-Esteem
When posts receive few reactions, adolescents may feel rejected, useless, or invisible. Conversely, strong recognition creates pressure to maintain an idealized image. This constant need for validation weakens authentic self-esteem.
Identity Construction Through Others' Eyes
Personal identity can be shaped by others' perceptions rather than internal values. Young people adapt their behavior, appearance, and opinions to match what is most appreciated online, complicating the development of a stable and authentic identity.
